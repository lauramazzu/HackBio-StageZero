**Can AI Discover New Genes We Didn’t Know Existed?**
=====================================================

**Introduction**
----------------

Less than 2% of the human genome codes for proteins, while the remaining 98% consists of non-coding DNA, often referred to as the genome’s “dark matter” because its functions are still not fully understood (Yandell and Ence, 2012; Laurent et al, 2019). For many years, this large portion of the genome was considered “junk,” but recent research has shown that it plays an important role in controlling how genes are turned on and off. These regions include regulatory elements such as enhancers and promoters, as well as non-coding RNAs that regulate gene activity and help the genome respond to environmental factors such as stress, diet, and disease. 

Although it is crucial, understanding this part of the genome is not easy because many elements interact with each other in complex and not fully understood ways. Regulatory elements can act over long distances, making it challenging to identify which regions are truly functional. Much of this “dark matter” therefore remains poorly understood. This raises an important question: can artificial intelligence help uncover hidden genomic elements, or even discover genes that we did not know existed?

Artificial intelligence is now offering new ways to explore these questions. Machine learning models can analyze large amounts of genomic data and detect patterns that are difficult for humans to identify. For example, recent work has shown that AI can identify repetitive DNA sequences that are linked to cancer but were previously overlooked (Velculescu et al., 2023). More advanced models can also predict how DNA sequences influence gene activity across the genome (Google DeepMind, 2025). These developments suggest that AI may help reveal new functional elements and deepen our understanding of the genome.

**Main Body**
-------------

To understand how artificial intelligence (AI) can help discover new genes, it is important to first recognize the limits of traditional methods. For many years, scientists have identified genes mainly by comparing DNA sequences to those already known. While this approach has been effective, it often misses genes that do not follow familiar patterns. As a result, some parts of the genome may have been overlooked simply because they look different from what researchers expect (Libbrecht & Noble, 2015).

AI, especially machine learning, allows researchers to examine large amounts of genomic data in ways that were not possible before. Instead of focusing only on similarity, these systems learn patterns from data, including sequence structure, evolutionary conservation, and functional signals. This helps them predict new genes or functional regions that might otherwise remain hidden (Eraslan et al., 2019). This method is particularly relevant when studying amino acids like glutamine. While glutamine is widely known as a building block of proteins, it also plays a key role in cellular metabolism, including energy production and nitrogen balance. Some cells, especially cancer cells, heavily depend on glutamine to grow and survive. By using AI on genomic and metabolic data, researchers are starting to identify genes that may have unknown roles in glutamine-related pathways, such as those involved in transport and regulation (Pavlova & Thompson, 2016).

Another benefit of AI is its ability to combine different types of biological data. Machine learning models can merge genomic data with gene expression and protein information to give a more complete picture of gene function. For instance, a gene that seems inactive based solely on its DNA sequence might actually become active under certain conditions, which AI can help uncover (Rives et al., 2021).

However, AI has limitations. Its predictions still must be confirmed through laboratory experiments, and its accuracy relies on the quality of the data used. If the data is incomplete or biased, the findings may not accurately represent biological reality (Libbrecht & Noble, 2015). This can happen when AI models are trained on non-representative datasets (e.g., only on European groups), disproportionately affecting underrepresented groups (Abay et al., 2020). 

**Conclusion**
--------------

In summary, machine learning models can help uncover elements within the genome that were previously overlooked. By detecting patterns in large datasets, it reveals that biology is more complex than a simple list of genes. However, as it stands, its predictions may still require experimental validation and strongly depend on the quality of the data. Overall, AI is expanding our knowledge of the genome and changing how we study and understand biology.
